How to Locate and Catch the Top 3

Tarpon

Tarpon (Megalops atlanticus) are prized sport fish known for their strength, size, and acrobatic jumps. Found in warm coastal waters and estuaries, top fishing spots include Florida, the Gulf of Mexico, Central America, and West Africa. The best time to fish for tarpon is from April to October, especially at dawn or dusk and during incoming tides. Anglers use heavy tackle with live bait or lures, employing techniques like drift fishing, sight casting, and fly fishing. When hooked, tarpon often leap—requiring anglers to “Bow to the King” by lowering the rod to avoid breaking the line. As a protected species, they should be handled carefully and released properly.

largemouth Bass

Largemouth Bass (Micropterus salmoides) are popular freshwater game fish found in lakes, ponds, rivers, and reservoirs, especially around vegetation, structures, and drop-offs. They are most active during early mornings, late evenings, and in spring—especially during spawning season. Effective gear includes a medium-heavy rod with a spinning or baitcasting reel and 10 to 20 lb line. Both live bait (like shiners and nightcrawlers) and artificial lures (such as plastic worms, crankbaits, and spinnerbaits) work well. Successful techniques include Texas rigging, skipping jigs, and working lures near cover. Using sonar and watching for surface activity can help locate feeding bass. Catch-and-release is encouraged for conservation.

Red Drum

Red Drum (Sciaenops ocellatus), or Redfish, are powerful saltwater game fish commonly found in shallow coastal waters, including estuaries, marshes, tidal creeks, grass flats, and near beaches or jetties. They prefer 1 - 4 feet of water with access to deeper channels. The best times to catch red drum are during spring and fall, especially on high or moving tides, and around dawn or dusk when they are most active. Productive techniques include sight fishing for tailing redfish on the flats, bottom fishing with cut bait near structures, and casting artificial lures along grass lines and oyster beds. Known for their aggressive strikes and long runs, red drum offer an exciting challenge. Many states enforce slot size limits (typically 18 - 27 inches) to protect breeding fish, so catch-and-release is encouraged for larger bull redfish to support population health.
