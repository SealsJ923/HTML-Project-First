Tarpon

Tarpon (Megalops atlanticus) are highly migratory fish that thrive in warm coastal waters, estuaries, bays, and river mouths, favoring shallow flats, deep channels, and mangrove-lined shorelines. Some of the best locations to find tarpon include Florida's Boca Grande Pass, the Everglades, Key West, and Tampa Bay, as well as the Gulf of Mexico, Texas, Louisiana, South Carolina, and Georgia. International hotspots include Mexico's Yucatán Peninsula, Costa Rica, Belize, Puerto Rico, and West Africa (Gabon and Angola), where some of the world's largest tarpon are caught. The best time to target tarpon is between April and October, especially during early morning and late evening, when they are most active. By targeting the right locations, monitoring tides, and recognizing key signs of tarpon presence, anglers can significantly improve their chances of hooking into one of the most thrilling sport fish in the world.

Largemouth Bass

Largemouth Bass (Micropterus salmoides) are highly adaptable freshwater predators that thrive in lakes, ponds, rivers, reservoirs, and backwater areas across North America. They prefer warm, calm waters with plenty of cover, such as submerged vegetation, fallen trees, docks, lily pads, and rock structures, where they ambush prey. Anglers should look for schools of baitfish, surface disturbances, and jumping fish, as these indicate active bass feeding zones. Using fish finders and sonar technology can help pinpoint bass suspended in deeper water. By understanding seasonal movements and targeting key structures, anglers can consistently locate and catch largemouth bass throughout the year.

Red Drum

Red Drum (Sciaenops ocellatus), commonly known as Redfish, prefer shallow waters (1-4 feet) with access to deeper channels. The best time to locate red drum is during spring and fall, especially during high tide or moving tides, when baitfish and crabs are flushed into feeding areas. Early morning and late evening are prime times, as redfish are more active and less pressured by anglers. Popular techniques include sight fishing on the flats, where anglers look for tailing redfish, bottom fishing with cut bait near structures, and working artificial lures along grass lines and oyster beds. When hooked, red drum are known for their hard-hitting strikes and long runs, making them a favorite among inshore anglers. Many states have slot size limits (usually 18-27 inches) to protect breeding populations, so practicing catch-and-release for larger bull redfish helps sustain the species.
